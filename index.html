Indians removed from electoral rolls, and counting

The Machine That Erases You

Tomorrow, someone will have to prove they exist.

It might be you.

Read

In December 2025, a woman named Aheda Khatun was walked across the Bangladesh border and left there. Not deported to a homeland. Exiled to a country she had never once set foot in. She had lived her whole life in Assam, in the same district her parents had. A tribunal in Nagaon had decided in 2019 that she could not prove the link to those parents, never mind that four separate voter lists carried their names next to hers. Six years of appeals later, a High Court threw the case out, not because her documents were bad, but because she filed too late. Nobody read the documents. She is still in Bangladesh. Her brother is arguing her case now, because she cannot.

That is not a glitch in the system. It is the system working exactly as designed.

The High Court never weighed any of it, it ruled on a filing delay and moved on. She was deported to a country she had spent exactly zero days in.

Two women are sitting in that camp today under an order that runs out on the date above. Saleha Khatun, 50, cannot read or write. Sarbhanu Begum's own name shows up spelled three different ways across her own government records, "Sarbhanu," "Sorbhanu," "Saharbhanu," and that inconsistency is being used against her. A vacation bench bought them a few more weeks. Whether it becomes a few more years depends on what happens on the clock above.

On May 27 the Supreme Court looked at the machine feeding that camp and told the entire country to go ahead and build one too. Full marks for constitutionality, basically. Then it waved the thing into sixteen more states. Delhi's version started June 30. So did Karnataka's. Right now, while you're reading this, something close to four lakh officers are going door to door across India making people prove who they are. Phase III alone is 36.73 crore people. By December, everywhere except Himachal, Jammu and Kashmir, and Ladakh gets the same knock.

Figures are net or gross deletions as reported by the Election Commission and state Chief Electoral Officers at each stage. Cut-off dates and methodology differ by state. The Commission's own national running count, gross rather than net, is nearing six crore, with over 5.18 crore removed in the second phase alone. Phase III is still underway and will add more.

Officially this is housekeeping. Clean the rolls, cut the duplicates, nothing to see here. In practice the 2003 electoral roll turned into a wall. Can't trace your name, or your father's, back to it? You get one month, and an enumerator with a clipboard gets to decide whether whatever you bring is good enough. The official term for most of what gets caught is "logical discrepancy," which is government-speak for a spelling mismatch, or a name that got transliterated one way in 2003 and another way in 2024, or your own father's name written differently by two clerks who never met each other, forty years apart. None of it your fault. All of it now your problem.

The Election Commission has a word for the whole thing: purification. Say that out loud. A voter list is not supposed to decide whether you get to keep being a citizen. This one just does, casually, and calls it cleaning.

How one deletion travels
1
Your name gets flagged. A spelling mismatch, a missing 2003 entry, a father's name recorded two different ways. The system calls it a "logical discrepancy."
2
You get one month. Bring documents an enumerator may or may not accept, ask Aheda Khatun's family what happened to a certified gift deed rejected over a state emblem.
3
You are deleted. The voter ID your passport, bank KYC, and ration card quietly lean on stops working, all at once, without warning.
4
The deletion becomes a doubt. Per the May 27 order, flagged names can be referred to the Citizenship Act's Competent Authority, the same track that in Assam runs straight into a Foreigners Tribunal.
5
The burden flips. Not the state's job to prove you don't belong. Your job to prove you do, with paperwork decades old, against a tribunal backlog Bengal's own High Court put at 21 years.
6
If you lose, or never even find out you were on trial: detention, and in Assam, deportation to a country you may have never set foot in.

The trap, stated plainly

You need to prove citizenship to get a passport. A passport, by the government's own admission, does not prove citizenship. The tribunals that could settle the question have a backlog a High Court bench itself measured this month at 21 years. There is no fast lane out of that loop. That is not an accident of bureaucracy, it is the shape of the system.

What counts as proof, and what gets thrown out
A voter list entry, but only from the one date the Commission accepts
Village-head certificate, rejected in Aheda Khatun's case over a state emblem
Registered gift deed and land records, rejected over a spelling mismatch
Matriculation certificate, filed by R. Rajagopal, rejected with no reason given at all
Aadhaar, ration card, passport, none of these stands alone, each just points back to the others

The burden of proving continuous Indian ancestry sits entirely with the individual. A tribunal or an enumerator decides, one case at a time, which decades-old paper is good enough.

R. Rajagopal, former newspaper editor: missed his daughter's wedding waiting on a passport

Deleted from the Ballygunge roll in March over a "logical discrepancy" traced to a 2002 list. An adverse police report stalled his renewal. He missed his daughter's wedding in San Francisco on April 17, holding a valid ten-year US visa he could not use. It took an MP, a letter from one chief minister to another, and national headlines before the passport turned up by speed post on July 4. "I was an editor once," he wrote. "Today I am voteless, passportless, a football kicked around like millions of Indians." He did not sound relieved when it arrived. He sounded tired, and he asked the only question that matters: what about everyone whose name nobody recognises.

West Bengal didn't wait for an appeal. The moment a name got flagged, the ration card tied to it got cancelled, no hearing, no finding of ineligibility, just a flag in a database and a family suddenly buying food at full price. Somewhere between 35 and 60 lakh cards are sitting in that limbo right now. The Supreme Court's move was to send the petitioners back to the Calcutta High Court, the same court that has already told you, on the record, that its own appeals queue needs 21 years.

Adil Hossain studies citizenship for a living, which makes what happened to him almost funny, if it weren't happening to him. His own name got struck off the roll in Uttar Dinajpur. He had a passport. He had land records going back to the 1950s. None of it got there in time. His parents voted. His sister voted. He didn't. He's said plainly what this actually looks like from where he's standing, that tying a ration card to a voter roll doesn't tidy anything up, it sorts a country into people who count and people who don't. Not a new kind of voter. A new kind of citizen, or half of one. Say both words out loud and hear how different they are.

Two accounts of the same exercise, click to compare

Twenty-three opposition parties and an independent MP wrote directly to the Chief Justice on June 30, saying they had run out of other options. Congress's Jairam Ramesh has called the exercise "a vote chori on a gigantic scale." Trinamool Congress makes near-identical claims about West Bengal specifically.

The Commission says the goal is accuracy, stripping out dead, duplicate, shifted and ineligible entries, with a Booth Level Agent from every party watching the process. In Karnataka, Chief Minister D. K. Shivakumar has pushed back from the other side entirely, denying interference and inviting the Commission to probe any complaint directly.

Both sides are here because you deserve to read what each is actually arguing, not a summary filtered through the other.

What nobody disputes is the arithmetic. The Commission's own figures put the national total near six crore deletions, with 5.18 crore in the second phase alone. Even inside Karnataka's ruling coalition the process has turned into a fight, BJP and JD(S) accuse booth officers of skipping doorstep visits for camps at community halls and mosques, Congress says those camps exist because nobody can physically knock on every slum door in a month, and both sides have now asked the state's own election officer to investigate the other. When the people running the exercise cannot agree it is being run fairly, that tells you something the topline numbers cannot.

01a / The smaller fights

Three arguments buried inside the big one

Strip the politics away and three narrower fights remain. Each has already reached a courtroom.

Aadhaar. Bihar's SIR left Aadhaar off the accepted-document list on the theory that it proves identity, not citizenship. Petitioners pointed out Aadhaar is the one document almost every Indian actually holds, so excluding it excludes them too. The Court split the difference: Aadhaar stays off the core checklist but can be used later to file a claim. Chief Justice Surya Kant asked the question that should worry everyone, out loud, in open court: should a labourer handed an Aadhaar card to collect subsidised rations also get to vote on that basis alone?

The unexplained deletions. The Association for Democratic Reforms pulled Bihar's own Chief Electoral Officer records and found 9,968 of 3.66 lakh deleted names carried no stated reason whatsoever, not death, not migration, not duplication, nothing. The Commission says 99 percent of deletions are legitimate. Fine. What happens to the roughly one percent that is not, and to the people inside that one percent, is the actual question, and nobody has answered it.

Whose side the process is on. Justice Joymalya Bagchi told senior advocate Kapil Sibal, arguing for West Bengal and Kerala, that software catches duplicates but only a human catches a dead voter, and that whoever benefits from a dirty roll has every reason to keep it dirty. Fair point. It also landed, next to the Aadhaar exchange, as a moment where a routine cleanup turned, on the record, into an argument about who India's electoral machinery actually protects.

Between 1985 and 2019, a huge share of Assam's Foreigners Tribunal rulings went through with nobody in the room. The accused never showed up, usually because they never got told there was a hearing to show up to, and they lost automatically because the burden was on them and nobody bothered explaining they had a burden at all. A database flag. A hearing you never knew about. A deadline that came and went without you. A country you were born in, now asking for your papers.

That is the exact machine the May 27 judgment just bolted onto the rest of the country. Bihar voters flagged for "doubtful citizenship" get referred straight to the Citizenship Act's Competent Authority. The Court says, don't worry, this isn't a final citizenship ruling. Go read that line again after the first Foreigners Tribunal opens outside Assam. A deletion becomes a doubt. A doubt becomes a file with your name on it. The burden is yours. It is always yours.

A spelling mismatch from twenty years ago is now grounds to erase your citizenship. Nobody has to prove you're foreign. You have to prove you're not.

03 / The court

A verdict that arrived after it could still change anything

Lawyers challenging the exercise in 2025 warned the Supreme Court it risked becoming a citizenship filter dressed up as an electoral roll revision. The Court agreed the questions were serious. Then it let the exercise keep running while it thought about the answer. That one decision, to not press pause, decided everything that came after it.

For months the Court supervised the details in real time, ordering Aadhaar accepted for late claims, ordering district-wise deletion lists published, standing up appellate tribunals. All useful. All beside the one question actually in front of it: did the Commission even have the power to do this. Bihar voted in November 2025 on the roll this exercise produced. A government formed on it. Only then, six months after the votes were counted, on May 27, did the Court rule on legality, in a 124-page judgment that answered all four constitutional questions the Commission's way.

The order of operations is the entire criticism. A verdict that lands after the election it was supposed to govern has already happened is not a neutral verdict, it is a rubber stamp with a delay on it. Pausing the exercise until the Court actually ruled would have cost nothing but time. Not pausing it meant that by the time the answer came, the answer could no longer undo Bihar and could only wave fifteen more states through the same door.

One legal write-up on the ruling didn't pull punches, calling it thin analysis that waved through what critics see as an off-the-books citizenship screen, without ever asking why the actual Citizenship Act process got skipped. The Wire went further, calling it a new low for a judiciary it says has already lost the plot, and noting West Bengal alone lost at least 27 lakh voters under the Court's own watch, the watch that was supposed to guarantee this was being done fairly. A calmer, more academic take lands in the same place from a different road: the Court has spent decades handing the Election Commission more power without ever building the guardrails to match, and this ruling just keeps that habit alive instead of fixing it.

Here's the irony nobody's talking about enough. The same judgment says citizenship questions from the SIR need to be resolved fast, because leaving someone's electoral rights hanging hurts real people. Sure. Fine. That is also, by the Court's own logic, the exact reason it should have settled the bigger question, whether this was legal at all, before 5.2 crore names got wiped, not six months and one election after.

It doesn't know your name yet.
It will.
Door to door. State by state. Until it does.

Saleha Khatun and Sarbhanu Begum's protection runs out July 16 unless the Court says otherwise. Sirajul Shekh still cannot travel for treatment he needs now. R. Rajagopal got his passport back, only because enough people knew his name to make noise. Aheda Khatun is in Bangladesh, still. Five point two crore names are already gone. Sixteen states haven't even started.

A voter list was supposed to record who could vote. It has quietly become the document that decides who gets to stay a citizen at all. That machine now has a Supreme Court ruling behind it, a door-to-door survey running in your city right now, a 21-year queue waiting for anyone who gets caught in it, and not one person in power who has shown any intention of slowing it down.
