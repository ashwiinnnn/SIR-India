The Machine That Erases You

Tomorrow, someone will have to prove they exist.
It might be you.

Read

In December 2025, Aheda Khatun was pushed across the border into Bangladesh.

She had lived in Assam her entire life, in the same district her parents had. What she also had, since a Foreigners' Tribunal in Nagaon ruled in 2019 that she had failed to prove the link to her own parents, was a status the state uses instead of a name: Declared Foreigner.

That ruling set a clock running. Detention. A writ petition. The Gauhati High Court dismissed her case in August 2025, not on the merits of anything she had submitted, but because she was six years late in filing. Weeks later, she was deported to a country she had never lived in. She is still there. Her case is now before the Supreme Court, argued through an affidavit her brother filed on her behalf, because she cannot appear to speak for herself.

Delhi's turn began June 30. So did Karnataka's. Nearly four lakh booth-level officers are going door-to-door, right now, across the country. Phase III alone covers 36.73 crore Indians. The entire country, except Himachal Pradesh, Jammu & Kashmir and Ladakh, before the year ends.

Figures are net or gross deletions as reported by the ECI and state CEOs at each stage; methodology and cut-off dates vary by state. The Election Commission's own running national count, gross rather than net, is now nearing six crore, with more than 5.18 crore deleted in the second phase alone. Phase III (16 states + Delhi, Chandigarh and 2 more UTs) is still underway and will add far more.

Officially this is housekeeping. Clean the rolls, cut the duplicates, nothing to see here. In practice the 2003 electoral roll turned into a wall. Can't trace your name, or your father's, back to it? You get one month, and an enumerator with a clipboard gets to decide whether whatever you bring is good enough. The official term for most of what gets caught is "logical discrepancy," which is government-speak for a spelling mismatch, or a name that got transliterated one way in 2003 and another way in 2024, or your own father's name written differently by two clerks who never met each other, forty years apart. None of it your fault. All of it now your problem.

Across the states processed so far, a net 5.2 crore Indians have been removed, deletions of 7.2 crore names against 2 crore fresh additions. India's first general election, in 1951-52, had a total electorate of about 17.3 crore. The people removed so far are already close to a third of that founding number.

The Election Commission has a word for the whole thing: purification. Say that out loud. A voter list is not supposed to decide whether you get to keep being a citizen. This one just does, casually, and calls it cleaning.

How One Deletion Travels
1
Your name is flagged. A spelling mismatch, a missing 2003 baseline entry, a father's name recorded differently by two clerks. The system calls this a "logical discrepancy."
2
You get a month to respond. With documents the enumerator may or may not accept, as Aheda Khatun's family found out when a certified gift deed was rejected over a state emblem.
3
You are deleted from the roll. Your voter ID number, the one your passport, bank KYC and ration card quietly depend on, stops working.
4
The deletion becomes a doubt. Per the Supreme Court's May 27 order, flagged names can now be referred to the Citizenship Act's Competent Authority, a track that in Assam runs into Foreigners Tribunals.
5
The burden is now yours. Not the state's. You must prove, with paperwork often decades old, that you belong. Tribunal backlogs in Bengal alone run an estimated 21 years.
6
If you lose, or never find out you were being tried: detention, and in Assam's case, deportation to a country you may never have set foot in.

The trap, stated plainly

You need to prove citizenship to get a passport. A passport does not prove citizenship, and India's own Ministry of External Affairs has said so. The tribunals that could establish your status face a backlog a Calcutta High Court bench estimated, this month, would take 21 years to clear. That is not an accident of bureaucracy. That is the shape of the system.

He wrote about it himself: "I was an editor once. Today, I am voteless, passportless, a football kicked around like millions of Indians." When his passport finally arrived, he said he did not feel jubilant. It only took a phone call once the Editors Guild, the Press Club of India and national media had made his name impossible to ignore. He asked the obvious question in return: what about everyone whose case nobody picks up. For the 5.2 crore without a platform, there is no MP dialling the Ministry. The machine does not pause. It simply continues.

West Bengal didn't wait for an appeal. The moment a name got flagged, the ration card tied to it got cancelled, no hearing, no finding of ineligibility, just a flag in a database and a family suddenly buying food at full price. Somewhere between 35 and 60 lakh cards are sitting in that limbo right now. The Supreme Court's move was to send the petitioners back to the Calcutta High Court, the same court that has already told you, on the record, that its own appeals queue needs 21 years.

Adil Hossain studies citizenship for a living, which makes what happened to him almost funny, if it weren't happening to him. His own name got struck off the roll in Uttar Dinajpur. He had a passport. He had land records going back to the 1950s. None of it got there in time. His parents voted. His sister voted. He didn't. He's said plainly what this actually looks like from where he's standing, that tying a ration card to a voter roll doesn't tidy anything up, it sorts a country into people who count and people who don't. Not a new kind of voter. A new kind of citizen, or half of one. Say both words out loud and hear how different they are.

Two Accounts Of The Same Exercise

Twenty-three opposition parties and an independent MP wrote to the Chief Justice of India on June 30, 2026, asking the judiciary to intervene because, in their words, they had run out of other options. Congress general secretary Jairam Ramesh has accused the Election Commission of functioning as an extension of the ruling party, calling the exercise "a vote chori on a gigantic scale." Trinamool Congress has made similar allegations about West Bengal.

The Election Commission maintains the goal is simply accuracy: removing dead, duplicate, shifted and ineligible entries so that only genuine citizens remain on the rolls, and that every party gets a Booth Level Agent to watch the process. In Karnataka, Chief Minister D. K. Shivakumar has pushed back on claims of bias from the other direction, telling reporters the government is not interfering and inviting the Commission to investigate any complaint directly.

Both accounts are here because you deserve to read what each side is actually arguing, not a summary filtered through the other.

What nobody disputes is the arithmetic. The Commission's own figures put the national total near six crore deletions, with 5.18 crore in the second phase alone. Even inside Karnataka's ruling coalition the process has turned into a fight, BJP and JD(S) accuse booth officers of skipping doorstep visits for camps at community halls and mosques, Congress says those camps exist because nobody can physically knock on every slum door in a month, and both sides have now asked the state's own election officer to investigate the other. When the people running the exercise cannot agree it is being run fairly, that tells you something the topline numbers cannot.

01a / The Smaller Fights

Three arguments inside the bigger one

Strip away the politics and three narrower, more technical disputes remain, and each of them has already reached a courtroom.

The first is about Aadhaar. When the Bihar round began, the Election Commission left Aadhaar off its list of eleven accepted documents, on the reasoning that Aadhaar proves identity, not citizenship. Petitioners pointed out that Aadhaar is the one document nearly every Indian actually holds, and that excluding it would exclude them along with it. The Supreme Court agreed only partway: it let the Commission keep Aadhaar off the core checklist, but ordered that anyone left off the draft roll be allowed to use it anyway when filing a claim to get back on. During the hearings, Chief Justice Surya Kant put the underlying worry in stark terms of his own, asking aloud whether a labourer given an Aadhaar card to collect subsidised rations should, on that basis alone, also get a ballot.

The second is about the deletions nobody can explain. Association for Democratic Reforms pulled the Bihar Chief Electoral Officer's own records and found that of 3.66 lakh names struck from the first draft roll, 9,968 carried no reason at all, not death, not migration, not duplication, nothing. The Commission's public position is that 99 percent of all deletions rest on legitimate grounds. Whether that number holds depends on what happens to the roughly 1 percent it does not appear to cover, and to the people inside it.

The third is about the argument the Court itself had with the people bringing the case. During one hearing, Justice Joymalya Bagchi told Kapil Sibal, the lawyer appearing for West Bengal and Kerala, that software can catch a duplicate registration but nothing catches a dead voter except a human being checking, and that whichever party benefits from uncleaned rolls has every incentive to leave them uncleaned. Fair point. It also landed, next to the Aadhaar exchange, as a moment where a routine cleanup turned, on the record, into an argument about who India's electoral machinery actually protects.

01b / The Enumerators

The people running the machine are dying inside it

Nobody votes on a Booth Level Officer. They are teachers, anganwadi workers, panchayat clerks and junior government staff, roughly four lakh of them nationwide, handed a door-to-door enumeration workload on top of their regular jobs and a deadline the Election Commission does not move. Since the SIR began, a rising number of them have not survived the assignment.

West Bengal has recorded the most. Shantimuni Ekka, an anganwadi worker in Jalpaiguri, was found dead near her home in November 2025; her husband said she could not bear the pressure of the work and had been turned away when she sought relief from the local block development office. Rinku Tarafdar, a part-time teacher serving as a BLO in Krishnanagar, died weeks later, leaving behind a note that held the Commission responsible. Haradhan Mandal, a headmaster deployed to Ranibandh in Bankura, died the same month; his note, too, pointed to the workload rather than any personal circumstance. In Malda, two BLOs who were also schoolteachers, Abul Barkat and Anikul Alam, died of heart disease within weeks of each other; a third, Utpal Thakur, followed in March, his family citing the same anxiety among voters placed in the "under adjudication" category that had built up around his booth. In January, Hamimul Islam, a primary school teacher and BLO in Murshidabad, became the eighth BLO death reported in the state since the exercise began, his brother saying the mapping and unmapping workload had gone beyond what his brother could handle.

The deaths did not stay confined to Bengal. Karnataka's own house-to-house round, which began in late June alongside Delhi's, had already produced three deaths among government staff on SIR duty within its first weeks, two BLOs and a Village Administrative Officer, with employee unions blaming excessive workload and pressure from superiors for all three.

Two counts of the dead, and no agreement on either

An independent think tank, The SPECT Foundation, documented at least 33 verified deaths of BLOs across several states, attributing them to stress-induced cardiac arrests, strokes and suicide. Trinamool Congress, campaigning in West Bengal at the time, put the toll in that state alone above 50, folding in ordinary citizens who died of SIR-linked panic alongside the BLOs. The Election Commission's own counsel called the wider allegations of workload pressure baseless before the Supreme Court, arguing it was reluctance among some BLOs to perform statutory duty, not overwork, that had forced the Commission to consider criminal action against defaulters. The Bharatiya Janata Party accused the state government of politicising natural deaths for electoral advantage. At no point did the two sides agree on a single number.

"This is not how the system should function."

Gopal Sankaranarayanan, lawyer for TVK, Supreme Court, on a BLO who was denied leave for his own wedding and later took his life

That case was one of several described in a December 2025 application before the Supreme Court: anganwadi workers and teachers sent out on enumeration rounds before and after their regular working hours, FIRs filed against BLOs under the Representation of the People Act for allegedly failing to complete assigned work on time, and a young man who asked for leave to attend his own wedding, was refused, and died by suicide soon after. Kapil Sibal, arguing separately, told the Court the pressure on BLOs was not anecdotal but what he called a harsh reality, and pressed the point that the pace being demanded in a state the size of Uttar Pradesh was simply not humanly sustainable.

The Bench, led by Chief Justice Surya Kant with Justice Joymalya Bagchi, responded with what it called firm but compassionate directions: state governments were told to supply additional manpower and cut individual workloads proportionately, employees facing genuine hardship such as illness or pregnancy were to be replaced case by case, and the question of ex gratia compensation for the families of the dead was left open for a later stage. Relief, of a kind. It arrived after the deaths it was responding to, not before them, a sequencing that by now runs through nearly every part of this story.

01c / The Bench, Off The Cuff

Even the officers running the SIR got caught inside it

On April 24, 2026, in the middle of West Bengal's own two-phase election, a fresh irony reached the Supreme Court. Roughly 65 people who were themselves on election duty, their names and EPIC numbers printed on their own official duty orders, discovered that the SIR had deleted them from the very rolls they had been assigned to help clean. M.R. Shamshad, the lawyer representing the petitioners, argued the deletions were arbitrary and unexplained, and put the irony plainly to the Bench: the people conducting the election could no longer vote in it.

"This election, yes, perhaps they can't vote."

Justice Joymalya Bagchi, Supreme Court, April 24, 2026, on the 65 election duty officers deleted from West Bengal's voter list

The Bench of Chief Justice Surya Kant, Justice Bagchi and Justice Vipul Pancholi declined to intervene directly and sent the officers to the same appellate tribunals already carrying a backlog large enough that a Calcutta High Court bench, in a separate order that same month, estimated it would take 21 years to clear. Justice Bagchi framed the trade-off in terms of what he called the more durable right, staying on the rolls at all, over the immediate one, casting a ballot in the election already under way, and said the tribunal would pass appropriate orders in due course. Whether the tribunal reached all 65 officers before the second phase of polling closed on April 29 is not something the public record answers. What the exchange captured, in miniature, was the machine's basic shape: even the people the state deputes to run it are not exempt from what it does, and even they were told, from the bench, to get in the queue.

02 / The Precedent

Assam already ran this experiment

Assam's own intensive revision of electoral rolls creates the "D-Voter" (Doubtful Voter) category. Over 1.1 lakh people are eventually marked this way, a majority of them women.
2019

August 2025

December 2025

March 2026
Saleha Khatun and Sarbhanu Begum detained at Goalpara. The pipeline that took Aheda Khatun decades is now processing people in months.

Status-quo protection for four women expires unless the Supreme Court extends it. The clock above is counting down to this date.

Between 1985 and 2019, a huge share of Assam's Foreigners Tribunal rulings went through with nobody in the room. The accused never showed up, usually because they never got told there was a hearing to show up to, and they lost automatically because the burden was on them and nobody bothered explaining they had a burden at all. A database flag. A hearing you never knew about. A deadline that came and went without you. A country you were born in, now asking for your papers.

That is the exact machine the May 27 judgment just bolted onto the rest of the country. Bihar voters flagged for "doubtful citizenship" get referred straight to the Citizenship Act's Competent Authority. The Court says, don't worry, this isn't a final citizenship ruling. Go read that line again after the first Foreigners Tribunal opens outside Assam. A deletion becomes a doubt. A doubt becomes a file with your name on it. The burden is yours. It is always yours.

A spelling variation in your father's name from twenty years ago. And you must prove you belong in the country you have never left.

03 / The Court

A verdict that arrived after it could still matter

Lawyers challenging the exercise in 2025 warned the Supreme Court that it risked becoming a citizenship filter wearing an electoral-roll costume. The Court called the questions serious, and declined to stay the exercise while it examined them. That single decision, to let the machine keep running while the case against it was still being argued, shaped everything that followed.

For months, the Court supervised the exercise in real time. It ordered Aadhaar to be accepted for claims after the fact, told the Commission to publish district-wise deletion lists, and set up appellate tribunals, all useful, all necessary, and all beside the actual question in front of it: did the Election Commission have the power to run this exercise in this form at all. Bihar voted in November 2025, on the rolls the SIR had produced. A government formed. Only then, on May 27, 2026, six months after the votes had been counted, did the Court finally rule on legality, in a 124-page judgment that answered every one of the four constitutional questions before it in the Commission's favor.

The sequencing is the criticism, not a footnote to it. A verdict that arrives after the election it governed has already produced a government is not neutral. Legal scholars writing on the judgment afterward made the point directly: the ruling resolves the legality of an exercise that was, by the time the ink dried, already complete. Pausing the exercise until the Court had actually decided the question would have cost nothing but time. Not pausing it meant that by the time the answer came, the answer could no longer change anything that had already happened in Bihar, and could only bless what would happen next in fifteen more states.

Four Questions, One Winner
Can the ECI run a blanket revision like this under Section 21(3), across whole states, rather than constituency by constituency?
Yes
Can the ECI drop long-accepted documents, like ration cards and existing voter ID, from the proof list?
Yes
Can the ECI question a person's citizenship in the course of an ordinary roll revision?
Yes
Was the process proportionate to the goal of a clean electoral roll?
Yes

All four questions were decided in the Commission's favor in the same 124-page judgment of May 27, 2026. Critics have pointed out that the Court did not weigh this against its own 1995 ruling in Lal Babu Hussein, which held that a person already on the rolls is presumed a citizen and the burden sits with whoever objects, not the other way around, and that it did not explain why existing citizenship-determination procedures under the Citizenship Act were set aside in favor of the Commission's own method.

One legal write-up on the ruling didn't pull punches, calling it thin analysis that waved through what critics see as an off-the-books citizenship screen, without ever asking why the actual Citizenship Act process got skipped. The Wire went further, calling it a new low for a judiciary it says has already lost the plot, and noting West Bengal alone lost at least 27 lakh voters under the Court's own watch, the watch that was supposed to guarantee this was being done fairly. A calmer, more academic take lands in the same place from a different road: the Court has spent decades handing the Election Commission more power without ever building the guardrails to match, and this ruling just keeps that habit alive instead of fixing it.

Here's the irony nobody's talking about enough. The same judgment says citizenship questions from the SIR need to be resolved fast, because leaving someone's electoral rights hanging hurts real people. Sure. Fine. That is also, by the Court's own logic, the exact reason it should have settled the bigger question, whether this was legal at all, before 5.2 crore names got wiped, not six months and one election after.

But it is coming.
Door to door. State by state.

A voter list was supposed to record who could vote. It has quietly become the document that decides who gets to stay a citizen at all. That machine now has a Supreme Court ruling behind it, a door-to-door survey running in your city right now, a 21-year queue waiting for anyone who gets caught in it, and not one person in power who has shown any intention of slowing it down.
