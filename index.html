The Machine That Erases You

Somewhere in India, right now, someone is being asked to prove they are real.

You don't think this story is about you.
That is how every story like this begins.

In December 2025. Aheda Khatun was pushed across the border into Bangladesh.

She had lived in Assam her whole life, in the same district as her parents. That history counted for little. In 2019, a Foreigners' Tribunal in Nagaon, Assam, ruled that she had failed to prove link to her OWN parents. What came next was the label that the state used instead of her name: Declared Foreigner.

That ruling set a clock running. She was detained first and then a writ petition was filed by her brother on her behalf later. The Gauhati High Court threw the case out in August 2025, not on the evidence or facts, but because she was six years late. Weeks later, she was gone. Deported to a country she had never lived in. She is still there. Her case is now before the Supreme Court, argued through an affidavit her brother filed, because she cannot speak for herself.

Between 1985 and 2019, nearly half of all Foreigners Tribunal decisions in Assam were passed ex parte. Not because nearly half admitted they were foreigners, but because many never knew they had to defend themselves. The burden of proof was theirs. Their silence became evidence against them. It begins with a clerical mark. Then a notice you never see. A hearing you never attend. A deadline you never knew existed. By the time you learn your name has been called, your country has already decided you do not belong.

That is the machine the Supreme Court's judgment of May 27 2026 now connects to the rest of India. The Special Intensive Revision (SIR) was upheld and the Court directed that everyone deleted from the 2003 Bihar electoral rolls on grounds of "doubtful citizenship" be referred, within four weeks, to the Competent Authority under the Citizenship Act. The Court was careful to say that such a referral is not a final determination of citizenship. But that qualification offers little comfort once the process begins. A deletion becomes a doubt. A doubt becomes a proceeding. And the burden is yours.

The legal pathway now exists. The administrative machinery is already moving. Bihar came first. Now the Special Intensive Revision has spread across the country. Phase I and II have already covered nearly 59 crore electors. Phase III has put almost four lakh Booth Level Officers on the streets, going door to door across 16 States and 3 Union Territories to verify another 36.73 crore people. Only Himachal Pradesh, Jammu & Kashmir, and Ladakh remain outside the current schedule

Figures are net or gross deletions as reported by the ECI and state CEOs at each stage; methodology and cut-off dates vary by state. The Election Commission's own running national count, gross rather than net, is now nearing six crore, with more than 5.18 crore deleted in the second phase alone. Phase III (16 states + Delhi, Chandigarh and 2 more UTs) is still underway and will add far more.

The Election Commission of India, insisted that the SIR was officially a "housekeeping" exercise. The stated purpose was narrow: clean the rolls, remove ghosts, and protect the integrity of elections. The ECI had last conducted an intensive revision of Bihar's rolls in 2003. Since then, there had been rapid urbanisation, migration, deaths unrecorded, duplicate entries accumulated, and, the Commission insisted, the infiltration of foreign nationals onto voter lists.

5.2 crore names, gone, net. 7.2 crore deleted against 2 crore added back. India's entire founding electorate in 1951-52 was 17.3 crore. What's already been erased is closing in on a third of that.

In June 2025, the ECI notified a Special Intensive Revision for Bihar ahead of that November's Assembly elections. Voters had approximately one month to file fresh forms. Crucially, the 2003 roll was treated as the baseline of reliability: anyone whose name, or whose father's name, could not be traced in that 2003 list was required to produce additional documentation from a list of eleven approved documents.

In Bihar alone, about 47 lakh electors, roughly 5–6% of the electorate, were removed from the rolls after verification. In West Bengal, the figure exceeded 91 lakh, nearly 12% of all registered voters. Many were flagged for what the Election Commission called "logical discrepancies": a misspelled name, a differently recorded father's name, a transliteration that did not match.

The case of Congress candidate Mohtab Sheikh is the contradiction at the heart of this system. A passport holder, an Aadhaar holder, and a registered voter was removed because a database disagreed with the spelling of his father's name. A tribunal restored him. He won his election. But millions do not have the time, resources, or access to fight their way back into existence.

The Commission has a word for it: purification. But here's the thing, a voter list was never supposed to decide who stays a citizen. This one does, and the State calls it "cleaning".

The trap, stated plainly

To get a passport, you must first prove citizenship. But the passport you receive cannot conclusively prove that citizenship. The tribunals meant to resolve the doubt are themselves trapped under a backlog, a Calcutta High Court bench estimated this month that clearing it could take 21 years. The machine demands proof, rejects proof, and sends people back to seek proof again. The uncertainty is not a flaw in the system. It is a circle built into the process.

The burden of proving continuous Indian ancestry sits entirely with the individual. The tribunal or enumerator decides, case by case, which decades-old document is good enough.

That outcome matters. But not for the reason it appears to.

The lesson is not that the system eventually corrected itself. The lesson is what it took: a nationally known journalist, a viral letter, two Chief Ministers, a statement from Editor's Guild of India, a hundred days of humiliation. The question is not why Rajagopal eventually got his passport. The question is how many of the 5.2 crore others, the ones without the platform, without the lawyers, without a letter that goes viral, are still waiting. Or have stopped waiting because nobody told them they could appeal.

West Bengal did not wait for an appeal either. The state ordered ration cards cancelled for anyone marked absent, shifted, dead or duplicate in the SIR, before appeals were heard, before anyone was found ineligible for anything except a database flag. Between 35 and 60 lakh cards were at immediate risk. The petitioners asked the Supreme Court to intervene. The Court looked at lakhs of families about to lose access to food and sent them to the Calcutta High Court, the same court whose own backlog means those families will wait hungry long before they are heard.

Adil Hossain studies citizenship for a living. His own name got struck off the roll in Uttar Dinajpur constituency in West Bengal, weeks before the West Bengal election. He had a passport. He had land records going back to the 1950s. None of it arrived in time. His parents voted. His sister voted. He did not. "All of a sudden," he told Scroll, "the things I have read about are happening to me now." His read on the ration card order is the one worth keeping: the SIR was meant to draw up a list of the electorate; linking it to welfare benefits creates new categories of citizenship. Not new categories of voters. New categories of citizenship. Those are not the same thing, and the distinction is the ballgame.

A third account, from outside India

On May 1, 2026, three UN Special Rapporteurs, covering minority issues, freedom of opinion and expression, and freedom of religion or belief, wrote to the Government of India through the UN Human Rights Council's Special Procedures. Their letter cited the same 5.2 crore figure this article opened with, and pointed to Nandigram constituency in West Bengal, where by their account 95 percent of deleted voters were Muslim, in a constituency where Muslims make up roughly a quarter of the electorate. They asked New Delhi for a breakdown of the religion and ethnicity of everyone removed from the rolls, and noted that Article 25 of the ICCPR guarantees every citizen the right to vote without unreasonable restriction. As of this writing, no public reply by the Government of India has been made available.

What nobody disputes is the arithmetic. The Commission's own figures put the national total near six crore deletions, with 5.18 crore in the second phase alone. In Karnataka, the process has turned into a fight. BJP and JD(S) (alliance partners in the ruling NDA Government in the Centre) have accused booth officers of skipping doorstep visits for camps at community halls and mosques. The Congress Government in Karnataka says those camps exist because nobody can knock on every slum door in a month. Both sides have asked the state's election officer to investigate the other. When the people running the exercise cannot agree it is being run fairly, that says something the topline numbers cannot.

Three Fights Inside The Numbers  ·  Pick One

When the Bihar round began, the Election Commission left Aadhaar off the list of eleven accepted documents, arguing it proves identity, not citizenship. Petitioners countered that Aadhaar is the one document nearly every Indian holds, and excluding it would exclude them too. The Supreme Court agreed only partway: Aadhaar stayed off the core checklist, but anyone left off the draft roll could use it when filing a claim to get back on.

Association for Democratic Reforms pulled Bihar's own Chief Electoral Officer records. Of 3.66 lakh names struck from the first draft roll, 9,968 carried no reason at all. Not death. Not migration. Not duplication. Nothing. The Commission says 99 percent of deletions rest on legitimate grounds. Whether that holds depends on the roughly 1 percent it does not appear to cover, and the people inside it.

Justice Joymalya Bagchi told Kapil Sibal, arguing for West Bengal and Kerala, that software can catch a duplicate registration, but nothing catches a dead voter except a human checking, and whichever party benefits from uncleaned rolls has every incentive to leave them uncleaned. Fair point. It also turned a routine cleanup into an argument, on the record, about who India's electoral machinery actually protects.

All three arguments were live in front of the same bench, in the same set of hearings.

That case was one of several described in a December 2025 application before the Supreme Court: anganwadi workers and teachers sent out on enumeration rounds before and after their regular hours, FIRs filed against BLOs under the Representation of the People Act for allegedly missing deadlines, and a young man who asked for leave to attend his own wedding, was refused, and died by suicide soon after. Kapil Sibal told the Court the pressure on BLOs was not anecdotal. The pace demanded in a state the size of Uttar Pradesh, he said, was simply not humanly sustainable.

The Bench, led by Chief Justice Surya Kant with Justice Joymalya Bagchi, ordered states to supply additional manpower and cut individual workloads, replace employees facing genuine hardship such as illness or pregnancy, and left the question of compensation for the families of the dead open for later. Relief, of a kind. It arrived after the deaths it was responding to, not before them.

By supervising implementation before ruling on legality, the Court made it structurally impossible to rule against the exercise by the time it finally did. A sitting government had already been formed. Pausing until the Court decided would have cost nothing but time; not pausing meant the ruling, once it came, could no longer change what had already happened in Bihar, it could only bless what happens next in fifteen more states.
Four Questions Before the Supreme Court
Can the ECI run a blanket revision under Section 21(3), across whole states, rather than constituency by constituency?
Yes
Can the ECI drop long-accepted documents, like ration cards and existing voter ID, from the proof list?
Yes
Can the ECI question a person's citizenship in the course of an ordinary roll revision?
Yes
Was the process proportionate to the goal of a clean electoral roll?

All four questions were decided in the Commission's favor in the same 124-page judgment of May 27, 2026. Critics have pointed out that the Court did not weigh this against its own 1995 ruling in Lal Babu Hussein, which held that a person already on the rolls is presumed a citizen and the burden sits with whoever objects, not the other way around, and that it did not explain why existing citizenship-determination procedures under the Citizenship Act were set aside in favor of the Commission's own method.

One legal write-up on the ruling called it "thin analysis" that waved through what critics see as an off-the-books citizenship screen, without asking why the actual Citizenship Act process got skipped. A calmer, more academic take lands in the same place from a different road: the Court has spent decades handing the Election Commission more power without building the guardrails to match, and this ruling keeps that habit alive.

A new low for a judiciary that has already lost the plot.

The Wire, on the Supreme Court's May 27 2026 ruling upholding the SIR

The same judgment says citizenship questions from the SIR need to be resolved fast, because leaving someone's electoral rights hanging hurts real people. By the Court's own logic, that is also the reason it should have settled the bigger question, whether this was legal at all, before 5.2 crore names got wiped, not six months and one election after.

The Supreme Court observed, in its judgment, that accurate electoral rolls are the foundation of democracy. That is true. But the foundation of democracy is not only the accuracy of a list. It is the presumption that the people on the list, and the people not on it yet, are citizens until proven otherwise. It is the principle that the burden of proof belongs to the State, not to the individual who must prove their own existence. That presumption has been quietly inverted.

The SIR did not announce itself as a citizenship exercise. The ECI insisted, repeatedly, that it was about cleaning rolls. The Supreme Court accepted that characterisation. But the passport offices did not get the memo. The food distribution departments did not get the memo. The citizenship referral mechanism in the judgment itself suggests the Court, in the end, did not fully believe its own framing either.

Democracies do not typically lose credibility through a single dramatic rupture. They lose it through the accumulation of small administrative decisions, each individually defensible, that together produce a population of people who must spend their days proving they exist. That is where we are right now.

The Court's job, in a constitutional democracy, is to ask difficult questions of the executive before harm becomes irreversible, not to validate an outcome after the fact, while offering those harmed the consolation that they can vote in the next election.

This was one of those moments. The Court was present. It was hearing arguments, issuing directions, engaging with the evidence. But it did not answer the question that needed to be answered before Bihar voted.

Insufficient Proof

This is the standard 5.2 crore people were just held to. Most of them did not have an answer either.

The Machine does not know your name yet.
But it is coming.
Door to door. State by state.

Saleha Khatun and Sarbhanu Begum's protection runs out July 16 2026, unless the Court says otherwise. Sirajul Shekh still can't travel for treatment. R. Rajagopal got his passport back, because his name was well-known enough to matter. Aheda Khatun is in Bangladesh. Five point two crore names, already gone. Sixteen states, still to come.

A voter list was supposed to record who could vote. It has quietly become the document deciding who gets to stay a citizen at all. That machine now has a Supreme Court ruling behind it. A door-to-door survey running in your city right now. A 21-year queue waiting for anyone caught in it. And not one person in power who's shown any sign of slowing it down.
