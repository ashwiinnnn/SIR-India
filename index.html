/ 01 / INSIDE THE COURT

Three arguments inside the bigger one

The petitioners had argued across three distinct legal grounds, and the Court heard them all differently. Each revealed something about how the State sees itself, and by extension, what it believes it can do to its own citizens.

The first is routine—technical objection. The arguments here spun around the statutory limit of duplicate entries versus the administrative limit of verification time. Could an algorithm flag a duplicate faster than human checking could confirm a death? The State's answer was implicit: no. So it asked instead: how many names must we check, and by when?

The second was procedural. Notice. Publication. The statutory window. The petitioners argued that Form 6, the application every fresh voter must file, created a threshold below which the State could not revise. The State's answer, delivered through counsel, was more elegant. It said: we can revise without waiting for Form 6. We need only the occasion—a list, a reason, a deadline. Form 6 is not a trigger. Form 6 is decoration.

The third is the argument the Court had with the petitioners themselves. Justice Joymalya Bagchi told Kapil Sibal, arguing for West Bengal and Kerala, that software can catch a duplicate registration but nothing catches a dead voter except a human being checking, and that whichever party benefits from an uncleaned roll has every incentive to leave it uncleaned. A fair point, on its own terms. It also turned a routine cleanup into a live argument, on the public record, about who India's electoral machinery actually exists to protect.

AS THIS ARTICLE WAS BEING WRITTEN

The Election Commission has quietly rewritten the front door to the voter roll itself. A new declaration has appeared inside Form 6, the application every fresh voter must file, on the Commission's own ECINET portal. It now asks applicants to state their parents' status and record from the previous SIR round before their own name can be considered. The field is live only in states and union territories where the 2025-26 revision has finished or is underway, meaning it is expanding by the week. No separate rule, no notification, no public order accompanies it. A citizen's father and mother have become part of the paperwork required to prove the citizen is real, and the Commission has not explained, on the record, why an ordinary application form now carries a lineage test nobody voted for and no law appears to require.
